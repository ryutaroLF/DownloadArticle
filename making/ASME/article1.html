      A new kinetic Monte Carlo method for modeling phonon transport in quantum dot superlattices is presented. The method uses phonon scattering phase functions and cross sections to describe collisions between phonons and quantum dots. The phase functions and cross sections are generated using molecular dynamics simulation, which is capable of including atomistic effects otherwise unavailable in Monte Carlo approaches. The method is demonstrated for a test case featuring a Si-Ge quantum dot superlattice, and the model is compared against published experiments. It is found that molecular dynamics-derived cross sections must be weighted by diffuse mismatch model-type weighting factors in order to satisfy detailed balance considerations. Additionally, it is found that thin alloy “base layer” films strongly reduce thermal conductivity in these systems and must be included in the modeling to obtain agreement with published experimental data.

      Introduction

      Motivation.

      The development of new materials with nanometer-scale structures has opened up possibilities for achieving with very low thermal conductivity. Thermoelectric devices used for compact, reliable power generation and refrigeration may see major efficiency improvements using these new materials. Such devices require materials that permit electron transport but block heat transport [1]. In dielectric materials and semiconductors, heat is transported primarily by mechanical waves that arise from displacement of atoms from their lattice positions. These waves, which are quantized as phonons, have wavelengths primarily in the submicron range, and their transport may be strongly disrupted by structures with comparable length scale. The thermal conductivities of planar superlattices, nanowire-based materials, quantum dot superlattices, and other patterned materials have been studied both theoretically and experimentally with encouraging results for thermal conductivity reduction [2–5]. In particular, Si/Ge quantum dot superlattices have been shown to suppress thermal conductivity to levels well below that of either bulk material [6]. To develop and design quantum dot superlattice materials with optimally low thermal conductivity, it is crucial to determine how nanostructures affect thermal transport. Computational approaches for thermal conductivity prediction that incorporate the correct physics of phonon-nanostructure interaction are critically important in this regard.

      The Boltzmann transport equation provides a description of energy propagation via phonons but remains difficult to solve for inhomogeneous and nanostructured materials due to uncertainties in describing phonon interactions (collisions) with defects, inclusions, and other phonons [7–9]. It is still considered the most theoretically sound, basic framework for heat transfer modeling and may be used as a starting point for development of new models and governing equations [10]. Numerical methods for its solution include high-speed techniques such as the Lattice Boltzmann method [11]. Other numerical methods for simulation of propagation and scattering of phonons in nanometer-scale composite structures have been developed but by themselves are either impractical at micron length scales [12] or cannot correctly capture the details of phonon interactions with nanometer-scale inclusions [13]. For example, molecular dynamics (MD) approaches can be used to model scattering of high-frequency phonons from nanoscale inclusions of arbitrary geometry and roughness, but the computational expense of such approaches is too great to model the effective thermal conductivity of quantum dot superlattices of realistic size [12]. The atomistic Green's function method [14] is a promising technique for computing phonon interactions at boundaries, with applications in ballistic energy transport situations, but is not suitable for the diffusive transport conditions occurring in bulk materials. Monte Carlo (MC) models of phonon transport are often used in thermal conductivity calculations because of their manageable computational cost when simulating events at the nanosecond and micrometer scales. These models are based on similar numerical methods used to describe the transport of electrons and photons, and offer relatively rapid solution of nonequilibrium heat transport for a variety of materials and temperature ranges [15–19]. The key challenge in MC modeling of thermal energy transport is to select suitable descriptions of events that create, destroy, and scatter phonons.

      In this study, a new MC approach was developed to predict bulk effective thermal conductivity of quantum dot superlattices. The method accounts for phonon scattering from embedded nanoparticles by incorporating phonon scattering cross sections and phase functions calculated in MD models [12]. The MC model was validated against a published experimental test case [20,21], and the most influential scattering mechanisms were identified. Before discussing the details of the new model, we will first review key ideas behind MC thermal conductivity simulations and then review previous work in the literature. To distinguish this model from other Monte Carlo models used in material science and solid state physics (e.g., the Metropolis method [22]), it is more properly called a “Kinetic Monte Carlo Model,” though we use the terms interchangeably. The term “kinetic” refers to the model's use of a time variable, along with velocities, energy migration rates, and other time-dependent phenomena.

      Common Features of Kinetic Monte Carlo Phonon Transport Models.

      MC models of phonon transport describe phonon motions within a finite region of space with typical dimensions on the scale of microns. These models allow computation of thermal conductivity in materials with length scales beyond the phonon mean free path, and can also simulate ballistic transport at suitably small length scales. In the model domain, the frequency ω, energy ħω, and propagation velocity (group velocity) v⇀g are tracked for each phonon. The simulation progresses through a series of small time steps, usually set in the picosecond range. At each time step, the phonons drift through space in a straight line at their individual velocities. Various events may occur to create, destroy, alter, or redirect phonons, including Umklapp and normal phonon–phonon scattering process, collisions with grain boundaries, scattering by point defects, inclusions, or other interfaces and inhomogeneities. These are evaluated as follows. First, it must be determined whether a phonon has crossed a model domain boundary or fixed grain boundary during the time step. If so, the phonon is reflected in either a specular or diffuse fashion [23]. Next, all other scattering events are evaluated using a total scattering probability and a random number generator. The probability that a phonon experiences a scattering event in a time step of duration Δt is given as
      Pscatter=1-exp(-Δtτ )
      (1)

      where τ is a phonon relaxation time, analogous to a mean time between collisions. τ describes the characteristic time at which a slightly perturbed phonon distribution will approach an equilibrium population distribution [24,25]. A fraction 1/e of the perturbation of the phonon population will remain in the selected mode after a time τ has elapsed. The inverse value, 1/τ, may be thought of as a phonon scattering rate for phonons with a particular polarization and frequency. We stress that the use and calculation of the relaxation time is an approximation, and has shown some error in comparison with more detailed, comprehensive momentum space scattering calculations [26]. Equation (1) predates the implementation of phonon Monte Carlo models, and is used in the fields of Monte Carlo modeling of high Knudsen number gas flows (usually called Direct Simulation Monte Carlo) as well as MC electron transport in semiconductors and metals. A more detailed description or derivation of this equation is provided by Jacoboni and Reggiani [27] showing the extension of the idea of probability of occurrence on a differential interval to that over a finite time step.

      While there is debate about its general applicability [26,28,29], the single relaxation time assumption is frequently used in thermal transport simulations due to its relative simplicity. τ is not constant but is a function of scattering event type, phonon polarization and frequency, local temperature, and any other parameters describing the details of spatial variation of material density, elasticity, and anharmonicity. The overall τ associated with a set of parallel processes may be found using Mathiessen's Rule
      1τtotaleffective=1τphonon-phononcollisions+1τgrainboundaries+1τisotope andpoint defects+1τinterfaces+1τquantumdots+…
      (2)

      chosen to include as many relevant scattering events as necessary. Each of these “1/τ” terms may be further subdivided and represented as the sum of inverse relaxation times for parallel processes within a category, e.g., phonon–phonon collisions may be separated into different terms for U- and N-processes.

      Heat flux is defined as the net amount of energy passing through a plane in a certain direction, per unit of time, within a given time interval. After n time steps, in a time interval nΔt, the total numbers of phonons passing through a plane of area A in the rightward (positive x) and leftward (negative x) directions are NRight and NLeft, respectively. The corresponding flux is then
      q=[∑i=1NRightℏωi]travelingin +x direction-[∑i=1NLeftℏωi]travelingin -x directionAnΔt
      (3)

      A positive value of q indicates energy flowing to the right, and a negative value indicates energy flow to the left.

      The actual number of phonons in a material region of interest is far larger than is feasible to handle computationally, so the simulations treat a small portion of the complete phonon population and generate time averages of various quantities to build meaningful statistical averages. Typical values of the scale factor W, which is equal to the real phonon population divided by the simulation's phonon population, are in the range of 103 to 106 for micron-scale domain lengths [30]. Extending to higher temperatures and larger domains will increase W, but this does not necessarily reduce the quality of model predictions if the simulated population and simulated time are both large enough to densely sample the phonon population in each cell [30].

      For any averaged property to be meaningful, the MC model must first be run until it reaches a steady nonequilibrium state in which the whole domain has been influenced by the time-imposed effects of the boundary conditions and the various internal scattering rules. It is then necessary to average over a large number of steps, typically hundreds or thousands of steps. The achievement of model convergence may be checked by tracking q versus time and verifying its asymptotic approach to a single value for multiple locations within the domain. If the model converges, the amplitude of fluctuations in q will become small relative to the value of q. The rate of convergence to a meaningful solution strongly depends on the details of the phonon transport and scattering rules. Seemingly modest changes to rules or rate constants may result in major changes in the convergence time. This is especially important when the model contains a large population fraction of high-frequency, slow-moving acoustic phonons, a characteristic of many real materials. Typical simulation convergence times range from 0.5 ns to 100 ns. Actual time step sizes must be selected to keep the single-step drift distance of the shortest-lifetime phonons well below their mean free path, meaning Δt < τ for all particles in the simulation. In addition the time step needs to be small enough so that a particle cannot drift over (jump) an entire cell during a single time step, so when using cells of length LX, it is necessary to set Δt < LX/vg for all particles, by either changing Δt or by changing LX and cell count.

      After computation of Pscatter for a single phonon during a time step, a random number is selected between 0 and 1, and compared to Pscatter. If the random number is less than the scattering probability, then the phonon will scatter. Finally, the type of scattering event is then randomly determined using τ-weighted equations, as detailed by Chen et al. [31].

      Once the model has progressed through many scattering calculations and reached a steady heat flux, information about heat transport may be extracted. Heat transport is modeled in MC by dividing the domain into cells, applying desired temperature conditions at the boundaries, and evaluating properties such as local temperature within each cell and local heat flux at each cell boundary. In addition to observing the time dependence of q, model convergence may also be tracked by examining the local cell temperature, which will reach a linear profile when diffusive transport occurs.       Klitsner et al. [15] tracked a multiple-mode phonon population at low temperatures, with one available frequency per mode, and radiative boundaries. The high-temperature model of Peterson [16] used thermal baths, variable frequencies for a single phonon mode, and introduced cell pseudotemperature, which is the temperature of a Planck distribution of phonons having the same energy density (Σ[ħωi]/V) as that of the cell. Mazumder and Majumdar [30] simulated multiple modes and multiple frequencies of phonons, including energy conservation, with scattering rates based on trends published by Holland [33]. They also included a simplified impurity scattering equation proposed by Majumdar [34]. The scattering events in this model could change the population distribution by preferentially destroying high-frequency incoming phonons, without imposing such a preference when selecting outgoing phonons.

      Lacroix et al. [35] followed this same approach and used scattering equations based on the work of Holland [33]. In this model, N-process collisions were simulated by changing ω; and thus, group velocity magnitude, but not changing the direction of the phonon trajectory. U-process collisions changed group velocity magnitude and randomly realigned the phonon trajectories. The model weighted the probability of phonon generation from a 3-phonon process by the same mode and frequency-based weighting used to evaluate the likelihood of a phonon entering a 3-phonon process, preserving the original Bose–Einstein distribution.

      Chen et al. [31] extended Mazumder’s model and included boundary and isotope scattering, plus a genetic algorithm (GA) to generate and select a set of phonons yielded by 3-phonon processes, while conserving the total energy. For the N-processes, the crystal momentum was also globally conserved by the GA. We found that this model did not preserve the Bose–Einstein distribution, had frequency-related statistical biases in its populations, and possible convergence difficulties.

      Jeng et al. [23] modeled Ge/Si quantum dots with MC, using a “gray-media” population-averaged description for every phonon and combined all scattering processes into one simplified rate. This same approach was used for the modeling work of Tian and Yang (published first in Ref. [36]). Randrianalisoa and Baillis [37] used the method of Lacroix with radiative boundaries, and the τ equations of Mazumder. Their model tracked phonons one at a time, until each phonon was absorbed, destroyed, or exited the model. A later implementation [38] included scattering at the boundaries of pores. Moore et al. [39] modeled a square/sawtooth silicon nanowire in the manner of Chen et al. [31], to study boundary scattering and possible nonreciprocal transport. Hao et al. [40,41] used parts of the model of Jeng et al. [23], with a full acoustic phonon spectrum, a frequency-weighting algorithm based on that of Lacroix, and scattering rates from the molecular dynamics work of Henry and Chen [42].

      Mittal and Mazumder [43] continued the work of Mazumder and Majumdar [30] and added optical phonons to the model, plus Lacroix’s weighting rules, and the U-process τ equations of Narumanchi et al. [44], which are based on the low-temperature equations of Han and Klemens [45] and which account for various three-phonon processes. Wang et al. [46] applied the approach of Chen et al. [31] to calculate the thermal conductivity of a tapered nanowire. Their sloped impermeable boundaries permitted both specular and diffuse scattering. Huang et al. [47,48] used the MC model of Jeng et al. [23] to simulate transport in Si-Ge superlattices via gray-media phonons. They modeled phonon scattering from interfaces using acoustic mismatch equations published by Chen [49], and a spatially variable heat flux at the model boundaries, necessary to prevent cross-plane flux. Masao et al. [50] developed a Monte Carlo model to investigate methods for three- and four-phonon collision calculations in a simple cubic lattice. Their model matched pairs of phonons to directly simulate these processes and their energy and crystal momentum conservation.

      Péraud and Hadjiconstantinou implemented a “deviational” Monte Carlo method [51], tracking only the portion of the phonon population which has a small deviation from the gradient-free (equilibrium) distribution. The method is most suitable for cases with small deviation from equilibrium, and offers reduced computational cost and possibly more rapid convergence than the phonon Monte Carlo models examined here. The method did not attempt to address 3-phonon processes and was substantially different from those described previously, making it a new type of model with a similar name.

      A Kinetic Monte Carlo Phonon Transport Model Using Phase Functions

      Model Framework Selection.

      The goal of the present study is to calculate the thermal conductivity of quantum dot superlattices by performing MC simulations that incorporate phonon scattering from nanoparticles embedded in a host material. The phonon scattering information is generated by detailed molecular dynamics simulations [12]. This type of approach has not been previously used in an MC framework. A clear advantage of this approach is that the inputs for quantum dot scattering do not require guessing of unknown constants of proportionality for relaxation times, the common procedure used to “calibrate” phonon–phonon scattering calculations. The scattering phase functions are calculated for interactions with a single inclusion, so the present approach is most applicable to cases with minimal dependent scattering effects. Each phonon-inclusion interaction is treated as a separate independent event, and phonons travel through the bulk material between encounters with inclusions that serve as scattering centers.

      The material of interest for this study was chosen to be silicon with germanium nanoparticle inclusions (quantum dots). Several of the MC approaches documented in the literature were considered and implemented to determine the best approach for the present calculations. A computationally intensive combining-matching technique similar to that later published by Masao et al. [50] was found to exhibit poor convergence to the proper Bose–Einstein distribution. The genetic algorithm (GA) described by Chen et al. [31] was programmed, and a number of model shortcomings were identified. In addition to possibly selecting phonons outside the proper frequency range, the “bit-flipping” technique of Chen et al. preferentially creates output phonon populations with a uniform frequency distribution, close to that of the density of states-weighted frequency distribution of the original phonon population. The phonons entering scattering events tend to have high frequencies. Thus, Chen’s model preferentially destroys higher-frequency phonons and replaces them with lower-frequency phonons, altering the population from its original distribution (the problem addressed by Lacroix et al. [35]). This statistical bias may be offset by tightening the tolerances on GA energy conservation. The present study showed such a tolerance adjustment within the GA model would carry a high computational cost. It was also seen that the GA algorithm redistributes energy between modes in ways that drive the population away from the original Bose–Einstein distribution. Chen’s frequency-bin division methods were found to be very practical and other aspects of the approach were used as a framework for model development.

      The selected phonon–phonon scattering framework was constructed following that of Lacroix et al. [35], with phonon generation events possessing the same weighting as phonon destruction events, thus maintaining the proper phonon distribution at a given temperature. The case presented in Fig. 2 had a model domain length of 0.5 μm, a temperature of 300 K, approximately 5000 particles per cell, and a frequency-bin width Δω of 773 Grad/s.       Phonon Characteristics and Domain Treatment.

      In the simulations, each phonon was characterized by mode, direction of propagation, frequency, and wave number chosen in accordance with published dispersion relations for the 〈100〉 family of directions in Si [54]. As a result, the material was treated as elastically isotropic, the same approach used in all previous Monte Carlo models. Phonons were allowed to have one of two modes: transverse acoustic or longitudinal acoustic. This common simplification was chosen based on the small amount of heat flux associated with slow-traveling optical modes. The contribution of optical modes has been estimated as 5–10% of the total flux by Goicochea et al. [55]. Following Chen’s approach [31], the wave number and frequency values for each branch of the dispersion relation were discretized into 1000 individual values. The three-dimensional domain was spatially discretized using a linear chain of box-shaped cells, which simplified the description of boundary scattering as shown in Fig. 3. LX represents the length in the direction of the temperature gradient and was typically set at 500 nm. Width and depth LY and LZ were typically set at 100 nm. Lateral boundaries were modeled as perfectly specular to keep phonons within the domain without changing the x-axis component of their velocity, thereby maintaining the same heat flux following the collision event. Periodic boundary conditions were also tried, producing the same resulting heat flux, as expected. The discretization into multiple cells enabled a parallel processing approach in which each processor core tracked scattering events within a single cell. The cell boundaries naturally defined surfaces at which heat flux could be tracked. In addition to this simple box-shaped 3D domain, the method could be applied using a domain of a more unusual shape, such as a curved nanowire, by programming wall boundary conditions representative of the preferred geometry.

      The number of cells varied from 8 to 50 in various simulations, with many thousands of phonon bundles in each cell (typically 5000 to 8000). Time steps were 0.8 ps, and the model calculations confirmed that this time step was shorter than the shortest relaxation time τ for all phonons in all cases studied. The cells at the ends of the domain were controlled as thermal baths at the selected temperatures (Fig. 2). The number of each type of phonon in each bath region was set using the Bose–Einstein distribution function at a chosen temperature and the appropriate density of states based on an isotropic material with the dispersion relation produced by Brockhouse [54]. Particles were successively added to a bath until the cell pseudotemperature reached its set value, with a total phonon energy tolerance of ±ħ times the maximum phonon angular frequency. The modeled heat flux showed a weak sensitivity to the magnitude of the imposed temperature gradient. Most runs used a bath-to-bath temperature difference of 40 K, yielding 320 K and 280 K as the bath temperatures. When the temperature difference was increased from 40 K to 60 K, the bulk material conductivity increased by only 0.7%.

      Phonon–Phonon Scattering.

      The phonon–phonon scattering rates were computed following Sharma and Rose [56]
      1τ3-phononLA mode∝ω2Tm
      (5)
      1τ3-phononTA mode∝ωTm
      (6)

      Here, the exponent m had to be determined from experimental data. As discussed previously, the phonon–phonon scattering equations previously used by others were based on descriptions of low-temperature interactions, with the exception of Peterson [16] who used a basic frequency-independent 1/T relation. While the equations of Sharma and Rose are still approximate, they provide a more detailed description of high-temperature scattering processes than those used in Peterson’s model. Here, the phonon–phonon relaxation rate equations were fit to the experimental data of Glassbrenner and Slack [57], giving the constants mSi = 1.3 and mGe = 1.15 for the 300 K to 500 K temperature range. Following the approach of Lacroix, N- and U- processes were judged equally likely and the equations were used for both process types. The constant of proportionality for LA phonon scattering used in Eq. (5) was 5.0 × 10−21 (s K−1.3). The constant of proportionality for TA phonon scattering used in Eq. (6) was 1.2 × 10−6 (K−1.3). These constants describe phonon–phonon scattering rates in the bulk-like Si material surrounding the quantum dots.

      The model of Sharma and Rose is not the only available choice, and has its own shortcomings. Density-functional perturbation theory-based work by Ward and Broido [58] showed a high-frequency Umklapp process frequency dependence with an exponent of 4, which they attributed to scattering with optical modes. The simpler model used here does not separate scattering rate by modes, and uses only an exponent of 2 in Eq. (5). In the materials investigated here, we found phonon–phonon scattering to have a much smaller effect on thermal conductivity than phonon scattering from quantum dots and interfaces. To investigate this sensitivity, we replaced our LA mode relaxation times with times sampled from Ref. [58], and found a decrease in modal conductivity of 2% to 10% for cases with quantum dots. For this reason, we judged this relaxation time model, while simplified, to be suitable for the present calculations.

      Phonon-Inclusion Scattering.

      Phonon scattering from nanoparticles was modeled using a computed single scattering cross section and a known scatterer number density ρ as inputs. The value of scatterer density ρ was set as a constant input in the MC simulations. The value of the cross section σ was computed using MD (per [12]). Separate calculations were conducted over a range of size parameters ka, where k represents the phonon wavenumber and a represents the scatterer radius. The trends for small and large ka are already well-understood, as discussed by Kim and Majumdar [59]. Geometric and Rayleigh scattering expressions were used in these regimes, delineated as k > 0.6 kzone edge and ka < 0.5. For the intermediate Mie-scattering region of ka, MD contributes new information, captured as otherwise difficult-to-compute cross sections. The MD-determined cross sections were then used to compute scattering rates (1/τ) for collisions between phonons and inclusions, as described below.

      The materials were modeled in MD using the potential functions of Stillinger and Weber for Si [60], and of Ding and Andersen for Ge [61]. Scattering cross section is a function of phonon mode, phonon frequency or wavenumber, and scatterer size and shape, all of which were tracked in MD. The MD calculations included effects of atomic-scale roughness, mode conversion, strain, and anisotropic scattering.

      From simple classical considerations, the rate of scattering of one phonon from a field of inclusions is equal to the product of the number of inclusions per unit volume and the phonon source volume intercepted by each inclusion per unit time, which is equivalent to the phonon’s propagation speed times the scatterer cross section. This rate may be expressed as
      1τquantumdot≈σρ|v⇀g|
      (7)

      where |v⇀g| is phonon group velocity magnitude, ρ is the number density of inclusions, and σ is the scattering cross section of a single inclusion. In practice, the model time scale is chosen to be on the picosecond scale, making the length of the intercept volume associated with a timestep (|v⇀g|Δt) at least an order of magnitude below the spacing between scatterers.

      Equation (7) has been previously derived for MC models, and is used in gas dynamics in a similar form (per Refs. [30] and [37], from Ref. [62]). This result is independent of the chosen arrangement of scatterers, but its utility depends on the assumptions that the phonons may be treated as discrete pseudoparticles rather than waves, and that the time steps are short (Δt ⪡ τ). When these assumptions are valid, Eqs. (1) and (7) together yield small scattering probabilities for the great majority of phonons (P well below 10%).

      The MD-based procedure for computing the cross section and the phase function information describing change in phonon trajectory are provided in Ref. [12]. Calculations of scattering phase functions in MD at nonzero temperatures are prone to large thermal-noise related error, as the underlying phonon-nanoparticle scattering is obscured by phonon–phonon scattering. The MD scattering simulations used here were instead run at near-zero temperature, using low-amplitude phonon wave packets to isolate the effect of phonon-nanoparticle scattering, while screening out phonon–phonon scattering effects. The clear separation of the two categories of effects is an underlying idea of this multiscale modeling approach. The method is limited in that it does not capture the small changes in wavenumber and cross section that could result from changes in bond stiffness and equilibrium lattice spacing at higher temperatures. For the materials chosen (Si and Ge) the bonds are quite stiff, and anharmonic effects such as thermal expansion are small at 300 K. We believe that any errors arising from the neglect of temperature effects on phonon-nanoparticle scattering are smaller than errors arising from the SW potential’s reproduction of the first-order elastic constants of the material, as discussed in Sec. 3.

      The chosen treatment implies the scattering events are independent because each scattering process is accounted for separately. Due to lattice strain and differences in material properties between nanoparticle and host, a distorted dispersion relation and an altered vibrational spectrum will exist in the vicinity of a quantum dot. For low to moderate scatterer density, this distortion is confined to regions close to the scatterer. For a very high scatterer density this distortion would be a significant effect and the present approach would have to be further modified. The MC model treats the material as a uniform bulk away from (between) the scatterers. The net effect of local lattice distortion upon the traveling phonon is captured in the MD calculation. The MD data points were monitored in the far-field scattering region (r >3 a) where the elastic constants were close to the bulk values [63]. This was verified by measuring (static) residual stresses at the start of the simulation, and verifying that they were at least an order of magnitude smaller than the stress generated by the waves traveling in the simulation. Upon approach to a scatterer, an incident phonon may experience many disturbances which could be classically expressed as changes in the combinations of velocity and displacement vector fields necessary for coherent wave propagation with a single group velocity (a single mode) [64]. These local effects were captured within the MD model and quantitatively described by the effective cross-section and the scattering phase function. The detailed MD model was used for the specific reason that it can capture these difficult-to-predict effects and distill them into a useful phase function and a cross section.

      Scattering Events and Preservation of Detailed Balance.

      Using Eqs. (1), (2), (5)–(7), phonon scattering probability was computed for each individual phonon. In the event that scattering did occur, the selection of scattering event (phonon–phonon, phonon-quantum dot, etc.) was chosen with a τ-based weighting to maintain the proper relative probabilities as parallel processes, proportional to 1/τ(event type), as detailed in Ref. [31].

      For scattering by quantum dots, mode conversion in the MC model was evaluated randomly, with the probability of conversion into each mode determined by the fraction of energy scattered into that mode in the corresponding MD computation. These probabilities were functions of phonon wave number k and scatterer size a, shape, and composition, and they were evaluated by tabular lookups as a function of ka. Once the final phonon mode was selected, its trajectory was rotated based on a phonon scattering phase function Φ. Φ at each value of ka was obtained by interpolation using results computed by MD at individual values of ka.

      Tests of the quantum dot MC model at 300 K were performed using initial conditions representing thermal equilibrium (no temperature gradient, Bose–Einstein phonon occupation of all modes). Surprisingly, it was found that the presence of the nanoscale inclusions shifted the occupancies of the phonon modes away from their original Bose–Einstein distributions “fBE.” At all frequencies, the final LA population (Fig. 4, “no weighting” curve) was larger than the population predicted from Bose–Einstein and the TA population was smaller. This effect was more pronounced at the elevated frequencies corresponding to TA mode wavevectors greater than half the zone edge wavevector in the [100] direction, and also became more prominent with increasing number density of quantum dots. The effect was clearly absent for frequencies greater than the maximum TA mode frequency, as energy exchange between LA and TA modes could not occur at such frequencies.

      This deviation of TA and LA branch populations from the Bose–Einstein distribution indicates that inclusion of raw MD-calculated quantum dot mode conversion probabilities causes different “temperatures” to be established in the TA and LA branches, which is not consistent with equilibrium conditions. We do not believe this arises simply due to inaccuracies of the SW potential compared to the MC model’s dispersion relation (from Ref. [54]), because the distortion of the phonon distribution function resulting from use of pure uncorrected MD results in the MC model is quite large across the whole LA-TA interaction range, not just in the 3–5 THz frequency band where the SW model shows its major weakness. The magnitude of the distortion exceeds that expected from mismatches in dispersion relation-predicted group velocity magnitudes.

      This unphysical outcome prompted closer examination of the MD scattering cross section results for the various modes. MD can calculate the scattering cross sections for a single incident phonon mode scattering into each possible final mode. Tests of the MD model showed the cross-section and phase function were not sensitive to the incoming wave phase. All scattering events modeled in MD were observed to be elastic. Calculations for plate or pyramid-shaped inclusions such as those in Si-Ge quantum dot superlattices showed that the relative cross sections (“branching ratios”) from an initial mode into a final mode were roughly equal for all final modes. This was true for both TA and LA incident modes. In general, the branching ratios are not exactly equal; they depend on scatterer shape, size, and roughness and also on the group velocities in the scatterer and matrix [65]. However, for particle sizes of the order of the incident wavelength (ka ≈ 1), the branching ratio could vary from 1 to 3 [66,67]. Our MD-computed branching ratios, calculated for the above ka range, are consistent with these observations and with general acoustic scattering theory [68].

      Thus, the reason that the final TA and LA populations deviate from equilibrium is not that the MD cross sections input into MC are incorrect. Rather, it is that they were derived from single phonon scattering events that did not take into account the differing population densities of the various phonon branches. The net effect was to cause an imbalance in the ratios of energy in output modes versus ratios of energy in the input modes, thereby altering the original Bose–Einstein distribution and disturbing the detailed balance. This was analogous to the difficulty addressed by Lacroix for phonon–phonon scattering in accordance with the principle of detailed balance [35].

      An alternative approach is suggested by the diffuse mismatch model (DMM) [69] used to model phonon scattering at interfaces. In the DMM, energy is preferentially channeled into those output modes with higher densities of states. It is shown below that detailed balance considerations applied to the present problem of phonon scattering from nanoscale inclusions leads to DMM-type weight factors that, when combined with MD-derived scattering cross sections, correctly maintain equilibrium distributions in both phonon branches. To accomplish this, we multiply Eq. (7) by the total number of phonons of a given frequency ω and polarization μ at equilibrium, and write the total rate of scattering in a narrow frequency band from ω to ω + Δω as
      Nω,μ(ω→ω+Δω)τω,μ-1=DOSμfBEΔωσμvg,μρ
      (8)
      where the terms are all evaluated at frequency ω for the mode of interest. The total rate of phonon scattering in the frequency band, from a given input polarization μi into a given output polarization μo, per unit volume, may thus be expressed as
      DOSμ,i fBEΔωσμ,i→μ,ovg,μ,iρ
      (9)
      Detailed balance considerations require that the rate of scattering from μi into μo in the governing equations must equal the corresponding rate of scattering from μo into μi. This leads to the following expression:
      σμ,i→μ,oDOSμ,ovg,μ,o=σμ,o→μ,iDOSμ,ivg,μ,i
      (10)
      Eq. (10) shows that MD-derived scattering cross sections for a given input polarization must be weighted by the reciprocal of the product of density of output states and output phonon group velocity to maintain equilibrium conditions. In the Debye picture with degenerate TA branches, Eq. (10) reduces to
      σLA→TA2vg,TA-2=σTA→LAvg,LA-2
      (11)

      in agreement with Refs. [67] and [70]. Equations (10) and (11) are consistent with Lacroix’s observation that the probability of phonon–phonon scattering for a particular polarization must be weighted by the number density of phonons present in that polarization [35]. They are also consistent with the DMM, which weights the probability of phonon transmission across an interface by the reciprocal of the square of the group velocity of the receiving side.

      For these reasons, the MC code was modified to adjust the MD-computed cross sections using the weighting shown in Eq. (10). The normalized phase functions and total cross sections were not modified, but the ratios of cross sections for each mode were adjusted in proportion to the mode’s probability of being the input mode for a quantum dot scattering event. Incorporation of this weighting in the MC model led to better agreement with the Bose–Einstein equilibrium distribution (Fig. 4, “DMM-type weighting” curve).

      Comparison to Experimental Data

      Test Case.

      A literature search was conducted to identify an experimental case against which the MC model could be validated. Thus far most quantum dot experiments have investigated their growth and their optical and electrical properties [71–75]. While some experimental studies of quantum dot superlattices have addressed their thermal properties [76], rarely have they documented the dimensions and structural configuration in sufficient detail to permit direct experimental validation of the present MD and MC computational approaches. The well-documented quantum dot superlattice publications of Khitun, Liu, and Wang (primarily found in Refs. [77] and [78]) do provide this information and were thus chosen for comparison. Although it presents fewer images and less geometric information, the related work of Alvarez-Quintana et al. [79] may also serve as a useful reference for future MC models.

      Khitun, Liu, and Wang used molecular beam epitaxy to grow stacks of alternating sheets of Si and a 50%–50% Si-Ge mixture. Upon deposition, the Si-Ge mixture formed isolated islands on the Si surface with a flattened plate-like dot shape on the order of 100–200 nm wide and 10 nm thick. The islands, described as containing both Si and Ge, were assumed to have the roughly 1:1 proportion delivered in the incident particle beams. Khitun et al. measured the thermal conductivity of a 22-layer stack at various temperatures, including the 300 K temperature of interest selected for this MC study.

      MD models of plate shaped scatterers with typical dimensions 0.5 nm by 5 nm × 5 nm were set up and simulations were performed to calculate scattering efficiencies, cross sections, and phase functions for incident phonons of various wavelengths. The characteristic length a used to describe the scatterer size was the plate half width. The results of the MD calculations were scaled up to simulate a quantum dot of 175 nm width. As discussed in Ref. [12], scaling is permissible when the characteristic dimensions (radii) of quantum dots within both the real structure and the MD model are at least an order of magnitude greater than the material lattice constant, because in that case the scattering results are not strongly influenced by the effects of atomic-scale roughness present on the dot surface.

The program randomly selected a number between 0 and 1, and matched its value on the vertical axis with a deflection angle shown on the horizontal axis. In addition to the weighted deflection angle, the azimuth angle of the scattering event was randomly determined from a uniform distribution spanning a full 360 deg. This is equivalent to rotating the phase function seen in Fig. 6 about its horizontal axis, with the surface of revolution forming a full three-dimensional phase function. The deflection and azimuth angles were used to rotate the original group velocity vector of the incident phonon. In this fashion, the scattering angle was weighted to account for the anisotropic scattering produced in the MD simulation.

      Scattering due to Acoustic Discontinuity in the Surrounding Film.

      Closer examination of electron microscope images of the experimental sample showed a feature usually ignored in the published discussions of experimental results. In between each layer of Si, some of the Si-Ge mixture remained as a thin but visible sheet on the surface in between the regions occupied by thicker quantum dots. This material was reported as 50% Si and 50% Ge, and had a typical thickness of 1.5 nm. This length is the same order of magnitude as the wavelength of many of the TA phonons in Si, as well as a portion of the LA phonons. This somewhat rough boundary should have functioned as an obstacle to transport of small-wavelength phonons, and so the MC model was expanded to include it. The presence of such a base layer is visible in the fabricated samples of other researchers, such as Fig. 1(b) of Pernot et al. [80]. Comparable films are also deliberately introduced in some fabrication techniques that use “buffer layers” to control epitaxial growth (e.g., Bernardi et al. [81]).

      The simulation technique for this “base layer” of Si-Ge was based on the work of Schelling and Phillpot [82]. It was in a sense, a one-dimensional version of the three-dimensional MD model used previously. As with the three-dimensional model, separate wave packet simulations were performed in MD for each input mode, and results were tracked by input mode and output modes. A long but narrow MD domain was constructed with periodic boundary conditions on four of the six sides. A wave packet of a chosen wave number and frequency was launched from one side of the material, traveling along its length. In the center of the domain was a film of 1.5 nm thickness containing both Si and Ge atoms in a random 50–50 mixture. The incoming wave and transmitted wave energies were computed by monitoring and integrating local heat flux in front of and past the base layer plane during proper time windows. As the real material had multiple layers, the MD model was also run with multiple films in series to capture effects of inter-film reflection and possible interference which could change the per-film transmissivity. Figure 7 shows an image of a domain with a single layer, plus a schematic of the arrangement of multiple films. The atoms in the Ge/Si film were randomly arranged to give a roughened 50–50 Si-Ge atomic composition in the film. The probability of a phonon encountering or crossing a base layer during a single time step
      Pcrossing=Fcoverage|v⇀g.·x^|ΔtSplanarspacing
      (12)
      equaled the magnitude of the x-axis component of its group velocity times the time step size, divided by the spacing between superlattice planes. The additional factor Fcoverage in Eq. (12) accounts for the incomplete area of coverage of the base plane, as regions of the superlattice layer covered by quantum dots have no separate base plane layer. The coverage fraction Fcoverage was estimated at 0.92 for the test case, based on images of the experimental structure. Transmission coefficients were calculated in MD for normal incidence over a range of wavenumbers. To allow off-normal incidence, analytical equations to adjust for increased reflection of mechanical waves with oblique incidence were incorporated into the MC model (per Crain [83]). For an off-normal incidence angle of θ rad, the ratio of acoustic impedances of the scatterer and matrix materials was modified by a factor of [vg ratio2 + (1−vg ratio2)/cos2θ]0.5, where vg ratio represents the ratio of group velocities in the two materials for the mode of interest.       The result was a computed thermal conductivity of kc = 13.6 W/m-K versus an experimental value of 15 W/m-K. The quantum dots alone were capable of reducing the thermal conductivity by a factor of two (140 to 70 W/m-K). The same simulation was run multiple times with different random numbers to confirm that the first two significant figures were truly independent of the initial random number seed.

      Sources of Error.

      The collective approximations of the MC model permit it to give an answer to one significant figure. Convergence error and variations due to different random numbers used in subsequent MC model runs were both estimated as less than 1% based on repeatability of the results with three significant figures of precision. Clearly, more important sources of error may include differences in the elasticity as modeled in the molecular dynamics program used for scattering inputs, the very general form of the MC phonon–phonon scattering rules, the experimental sample’s variations in quantum dot spacing and arrangement, and certainly the simulation of silicon as an isotropic material in the MC model.

      As quantified by Balamane et al. [84] and Jian et al. [85], the Stillinger–Weber models of Si [60] and Ge [61] have errors in the elastic constants when compared to experimental measurements, reported as up to 25% greater than experiment. In addition to large errors for zone-boundary phonon frequencies, Jian et al. found dispersion relation frequency errors greater than 10% for all transverse phonons in Si at frequencies above 3 THz, and for all phonons beyond 11 THz. For the MD scattering case, the interactions depend strongly on the acoustic impedances of the materials, defined as the product of the mass density and the group velocity magnitude. Comparing the stiffness values of Balamane et al. with experimental measurements [63], the low-k acoustic impedances in the MD model will have errors of 1% to 4% for longitudinal waves of various alignments but up to 15% when comparing [100] transverse waves. With an error in acoustic impedance of 15% an MD calculation is not expected to be accurate to two significant figures, as that would imply a total error below 10%. We estimate the potential function’s overprediction of phonon frequency, group velocity magnitude, and acoustic impedance as roughly 10%, resulting in a comparable 10% error in predictions of conductivity.

      The common approximation of the material as elastically isotropic also introduces errors in the group velocity magnitude. Spatial rotation in silicon changes the speed of long-wavelength long longitudinal waves by up to 11% ([100] to [111]), and transverse waves by up to 15%. The use of the [100] measurement set as the basis for the MC model’s phonon velocities, thus, skews the results toward predicting a lower conductivity than would be calculated in an elastically anisotropic medium. The contribution to the total thermal conductivity of a phonon mode is proportional to its group velocity magnitude. We estimate the speed of sound and kc error associated with this simplification as roughly 7%.

      Conclusions

      A new Monte Carlo approach for thermal transport modeling was developed in order to permit a detailed treatment of scattering from quantum dots. This feature has not been addressed in previous thermal conductivity studies. The approach, which incorporates phonon-nanoparticle scattering phase functions calculated using molecular dynamics simulations, was used to model thermal conductivity of a silicon-germanium quantum dot superlattice for which experimental measurements have been reported in the literature. The simulations demonstrated the need to use diffuse mismatch type weighting when incorporating scattering predictions from MD or conventional analytical models. An important finding of the simulations was the importance of the base layer. This often-overlooked film performs an important role in the reduction of thermal conductivity and its function as a thin grain boundary may further improve thermoelectric device performance by acting as an electron-permeable obstacle to phonon propagation.
